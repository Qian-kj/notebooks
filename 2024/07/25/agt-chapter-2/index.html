1. Introduction

The introduction argues that the credibility of Nash equilibria as predictions of rational behavior is undermined if these equilibria are not computationally feasible. The chapter stresses that efficient computability is essential for any solution concept in game theory, echoing the sentiment that if a computer (or a simulated group of agents) cannot find an equilibrium efficiently, neither can real-world agents.

1.1 Best Responses and Supports

  1. Best Response

    A player's strategy is a best response if it maximizes their payoff, given the strategies of the other players. In a Nash equilibrium, each player's strategy is a best response to the others.

    The section introduces a useful observation for understanding and finding Nash equilibria: a mixed strategy is a best response if and only if all pure strategies in its support (i.e., strategies that are played with positive probability) are best responses.

  2. Theorem 2.1: A mixed strategy is a best response if and only if all pure strategies in its support are best responses.

    Proof: Assume a mixed strategy is a best response, but contains a pure strategy that is not a best response. The player could improve their utility by decreasing the probability of this non-best-response strategy and increasing the probability of other strategies, contradicting the assumption. Conversely, if all pure strategies in the support are best responses, then the mixed strategy must be a Nash equilibrium.

2. Is the Nash Equilibrium Problem NP-Complete

The concept of NP-completeness is to classify the complexity of problems. Common NP-complete problems, like SATISFIABILITY, often resist efficient solutions. This section explores whether the problem of finding a Nash equilibrium fits into this category.

  1. Unique Complexity of Nash Equilibria

  • Existence Guarantee: Every game is guaranteed to have a Nash equilibrium, which contrasts with typical NP-complete problems where solutions may not exist. This guarantee fundamentally changes the complexity considerations.

  • SATISFIABILITY Problem: The problem that asks, given a Boolean formula in conjunctive normal form, to find a satisfying truth assignment.

  • SATISFIABILITY Reduction: If Nash were NP-complete, we could reduce SATISFIABILITY to Nash. However, this would allow us to solve SATISFIABILITY by checking if a Nash equilibrium satisfies an easily verifiable property, which implies NP = coNP, a highly unlikely scenario.

  1. Specialized Complexity

    Problems with guaranteed solutions, like Nash equilibria, require a more nuanced analysis than NP-completeness. This section suggests exploring alternative complexity classifications tailored to problems like Nash.

2.1 Nash vs. Brouwer

  1. Nash's Existence Proof

    Nash's proof of equilibrium existence relies on Brouwer's fixed-point theorem, which states that any continuous function mapping a unit ball to itself has a fixed point. However, this theorem is known for its nonconstructive nature, implying that while we know a fixed point exists, finding it can be difficult.

  2. Complexity of Finding Fixed Points

    Finding a Brouwer fixed point is computationally challenging, indicating that Nash equilibria, while guaranteed to exist, may be hard to compute. This difficulty aligns with the broader challenges of computing fixed points in various mathematical contexts.

2.2: NP-Completeness of Generalizations

  1. Generalized Nash Equilibria Problems

    When the Nash equilibrium problem is modified to lose its existence guarantee, NP-completeness becomes relevant. For example, determining the existence of multiple Nash equilibria or specific types of equilibria can lead to NP-complete problems.

  2. Theorem 2.3 (Gilboa and Zemel, 1989): Several generalized Nash equilibrium problems are NP-complete:

    • Existence of at least two Nash equilibria
    • Existence of a Nash equilibrium where a player has a utility above a given threshold
    • Existence of a Nash equilibrium with specific support characteristics

  3. Proof by Conitzer and Sandholm (2003)

    A reduction from SATISFIABILITY to a symmetric game demonstrates the NP-completeness of certain Nash equilibrium problems. By constructing a game where strategies correspond to literals in SATISFIABILITY, it shows that solving for Nash equilibria in this context is equivalent to solving SATISFIABILITY, thereby establishing NP-completeness.

  4. Summary

  • NP-Completeness Inapplicability: Nash equilibria always exist, making traditional NP-completeness unsuitable for assessing their complexity.
  • Brouwer's Theorem: Highlights the nonconstructive nature of Nash's proof and the computational challenge of finding equilibria.
  • Generalizations: When modifying the problem to lose the existence guarantee, NP-completeness becomes relevant, demonstrating the complexity of finding specific types of Nash equilibria.

4. The Class PPAD

This section explores the complexity class PPAD (Polynomial Parity Arguments on Directed graphs), which is central to understanding the computational challenges of finding Nash equilibria. It builds on the Lemke-Howson algorithm's existence proof by constructing a graph of combinatorial objects and analyzing its properties.

  1. PPAD and Nash Equilibria
  • Graph Construction: The vertices of the graph represent combinatorial objects (vertices of or sets of inequalities). Each vertex has either one or two edges incident upon it.
  • Path-Like Structure: The graph exhibits a path-like structure with paths and cycles (see Figure 2.3). There is always at least one endpoint of a path, the all-zero vertex, which must have another endpoint that is a Nash equilibrium.
  • Directed Paths: The paths in the graph are directed. At each vertex, edges are assigned directions consistently.
  1. Directed Graphs and PPAD
  • Existence Proof: The graph for Nash's theorem forms a directed graph with all outdegrees and indegrees at most one. Each vertex is easily recognizable and corresponds to a specific combinatorial object. This structure ensures the existence of Nash equilibria.
  • Paths and Cycles: The graph consists of paths and cycles. By starting from the standard source (all-zero vertex) and following directed paths, one can find Nash equilibria at the endpoints of these paths.
  1. PPAD-Complete Problems
  • Common Structure: Problems in PPAD share the structure of having a directed graph with paths and cycles, where finding an endpoint (sink) corresponds to solving the problem.
  • Examples: Finding an approximate Brouwer fixpoint and the HAM SANDWICH problem are examples of PPAD problems. These problems require finding solutions in high-dimensional spaces with guaranteed existence but challenging computational complexity.
  1. Implications of PPAD
  • Solving PPAD Problems: To solve a problem in PPAD, one must efficiently navigate the exponentially large graph to find sinks. This is analogous to solving NP problems by narrowing down to a solution among many candidates.
  • PPAD = P?: The complexity class PPAD might be equivalent to P, implying polynomial-time solutions for all PPAD problems. However, current evidence suggests PPAD problems are likely hard, akin to NP-complete problems.

4.1: Are PPAD-Complete Problems Hard?

  • PPAD vs NP-Completeness: PPAD-completeness provides weaker evidence of intractability compared to NP-completeness. If PPAD = P, then many problems thought to be hard, like finding Brouwer fixpoints and Arrow-Debreu equilibria, would be solvable in polynomial time.
  • Potential Complexity: Any efficient algorithm for a PPAD-complete problem would need to address deep combinatorial and algebraic challenges. The complexity of PPAD problems suggests they are unlikely to be easy to solve.
  • Mathematical Acceptance: Despite the possibility that PPAD might equal P, the prevailing hypothesis is that PPAD-complete problems are indeed intractable.
  1. Summary
  • PPAD Class: Characterizes problems with guaranteed solutions that can be found by navigating a directed graph of combinatorial objects.
  • Directed Paths: Paths in the graph represent solution processes, with endpoints indicating Nash equilibria or solutions to other PPAD problems.
  • Complexity Implications: PPAD-complete problems are likely hard, although proving this conclusively remains a challenge in computational complexity theory.

5. Succinct Representations of Games

The input to the Nash equilibrium problem is a description of the game. For large games, describing every utility combination is impractical due to the enormous size of the input. This section discusses how games can be represented more succinctly to manage complexity.

  1. Full Representation of Games
  • Two-Player Games: Describing a game with two players and and strategies respectively involves numbers. This makes such games manageable computationally.
  • Multi-Player Games: For players each with strategies, representing the game requires numbers, which is infeasible for large . This necessitates more efficient representations.
  1. Importance of Succinct Representations
  • Efficient Algorithms: While a trivial efficient algorithm exists for small (try all combinations of supports), the huge input size renders it impractical for large . Thus, efficient representations are essential for practical computation.
  • Interest in Large Games: Real-world applications often involve games with many players, such as markets and auctions. Therefore, succinctly representable multiplayer games are crucial for computational relevance.

5.1 Graphical Games

  • Graph Structure: A graphical game represents players and their dependencies as a graph, where the utility of a player depends only on a few others. This reduces the complexity from to , where is the maximum degree of the graph.
  • Example: A directed cycle on 20 players using the game matrix from Example 2.2 reduces the description significantly compared to a full representation.

5.2 Other Succinct Games

  • Sparse Games: Games where most utilities are zero. Representing these games focuses on the nonzero utilities, reducing input size.
  • Symmetric Games: All players are identical, and utility depends on the number of players choosing each strategy. This reduces the required numbers significantly.
  • Anonymous Games: A generalization of symmetric games where each player is different but cannot distinguish between others. Utility depends on the partition of players into strategies.
  • Extensive Form Games: Represented as game trees, where strategies are combinations of moves at each node.
  • Congestion Games: Focus on network congestion, where players' strategies are paths in a graph, and utilities depend on the load on edges.
  • Network Congestion Games: A more succinct form where edges of an actual graph represent player strategies.
  • Local Effect Games: Generalizations of congestion games, capturing local effects.
  • Facility Location Games: Studied in the context of optimal placement and interactions.
  • Multimatrix Games: Games with players each with strategies, described by utility matrices for each pair of players.
  1. Summary
  • Representation Challenge: The input size for Nash equilibrium problems grows exponentially with the number of players and strategies, making full representations impractical for large games.
  • Succinct Representations: Various methods to represent games more compactly are crucial for computational feasibility.
  • Graphical and Other Games: Different structures like graphical games and other succinct forms reduce the complexity of representing and computing equilibria in large games.

This section emphasizes the need for and methods of succinctly representing large games to manage the computational complexity of finding Nash equilibria effectively.

7. Correlated Equilibria

7.2 Correlated Equilibria in Succinct Games

  • Efficient Algorithms: Polynomial-time algorithms exist for finding CE in games with succinct representations. These representations reduce the complexity of input size, making computation feasible.
  • Theorem 2.6 (Papadimitriou, 2005): For any succinctly representable game of polynomial type, finding a correlated equilibrium can be done in polynomial time.
  • Theorem 2.7 (Papadimitriou and Roughgarden, 2005): Optimizing a linear function over correlated equilibria is polynomial-time solvable in structured games such as symmetric, anonymous, and graphical games with bounded treewidth.

  • Correlated Equilibria: Allow for coordinated strategies via a trusted source, offering a computationally efficient extension of Nash equilibria.
  • Computational Efficiency: Linear programming can solve for CE efficiently, providing practical benefits in large and complex games.
  • Theoretical Insights: The relationship between CE and Nash equilibria highlights the broader applicability and flexibility of CE in game theory.

8. Concluding Remarks

Chapter 2 highlights the deep connections between game theory and computational complexity, emphasizing the challenges and opportunities in finding equilibria in games. The results underscore the importance of developing efficient algorithms tailored to specific classes of games and leveraging correlated and approximate equilibria for practical applications.

8.1 Key Points

  1. PPAD-Completeness of Nash Equilibria: Establishing that finding a mixed Nash equilibrium is PPAD-complete raises concerns about the computational feasibility of using Nash equilibria in practice. This result suggests that for some complex games, finding an equilibrium may be computationally intractable.

  2. Relevance to Economic Practice: The practical significance of the PPAD-completeness result is questioned. The complexity result applies to artificially constructed games designed to encode Brouwer functions, which may not be representative of real-world economic scenarios.

  3. Comparison with NP-Completeness: Similar concerns were raised during the early discussions of the NP-completeness of problems like the traveling salesman problem. The same arguments apply: while NP-completeness highlights computational challenges, it also drives the search for efficient algorithms for specific problem classes.

  4. Positive Algorithmic Results: The PPAD-completeness of Nash suggests that efficient approaches should leverage special properties of specific game classes. Proving positive algorithmic results for these classes is crucial for making Nash equilibria practical in applied settings.

  5. Refinement of PPAD-Completeness Proofs: Over time, PPAD-completeness proofs may be refined to cover simpler and more realistic classes of games, leading to more applicable and efficient solutions.
