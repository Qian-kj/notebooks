1. Introduction

The chapter provides a foundation for algorithmic game theory, focusing on game theory concepts applied to Internet scenarios and computational issues related to finding equilibria: - Introduces game theory concepts in the context of the Internet. - Illustrates classical games and their Internet-based applications. - Sets the foundation for understanding algorithmic approaches to game theory.

2. Games, Old and New

2.1 Key Definitions:

  • Game Theory: The study of mathematical models of strategic interaction among rational decision-makers.
  • Cost Matrix: A matrix representation of the costs or payoffs for each player depending on their strategies.
  • Dominant Strategy: A strategy that is optimal for a player, regardless of the strategies chosen by other players.
  • Mixed Strategy: A strategy where players randomize over possible actions to optimize their outcomes.

2.2 Important Notations:

  • Nash Equilibrium: A set of strategies where no player can benefit by unilaterally changing their strategy. Denoted as ( s S ) where ( S ) is the set of all possible strategy vectors.
  • Correlated Equilibrium: An extension of Nash equilibrium where an external correlation device can recommend strategies to players.

2.3 The Prisoner's Dilemma:

  • Example 1.1: Two prisoners are on trial and must decide whether to confess or remain silent. The outcomes are:
    • If both confess, they get 4 years each.
    • If both remain silent, they get 2 years each.
    • If one confesses and the other remains silent, the confessor gets 1 year and the other gets 5 years.
  • Application: Illustrates the conflict between individual rationality and collective benefit.

2.4 ISP Routing Game:

  • Example 1.2: Two ISPs must decide on routing traffic through two peering points (C and S). If both choose the closer peering point (C), they incur higher total costs due to congestion.
    • Costs are represented in a matrix similar to the Prisoner's Dilemma, where choosing C is analogous to confessing, and choosing S is analogous to remaining silent.
  • Application: Demonstrates how individual decisions in routing can lead to suboptimal outcomes for both ISPs.

2.5 Pollution Game (Tragedy of the Commons):

  • Example 1.3: Multiple countries decide whether to control pollution. Controlling pollution has a cost, but not controlling it increases the overall cost due to increased pollution.
    • The cost matrix shows that if no country controls pollution, the total cost is higher for all.
  • Application: Extends the Prisoner's Dilemma to multiple players, showing the overuse of shared resources.

2.6 Coordination Games:

  • Example 1.4 (Battle of the Sexes): A boy and a girl must choose between attending a baseball game or a softball game. Each prefers a different game but wants to attend together.
    • The game has multiple equilibria where they either both attend the baseball game or both attend the softball game.
  • Application: Highlights the importance of coordination in decision-making.

2.7 Routing Congestion Game:

  • Example 1.5: Two traffic streams need to be routed through two connection points (A and B) to avoid congestion.
    • The cost matrix reflects the delays incurred based on the routing choices.
  • Application: An anti-coordination game where good outcomes require players to choose different paths to avoid congestion.

Summary:

Section 1.1 introduces fundamental concepts of game theory using classical examples and their modern analogs, emphasizing their relevance in internet-based scenarios. The section covers the Prisoner's Dilemma, ISP routing, Pollution Game, Coordination Games, and Routing Congestion Games, providing a foundational understanding of strategic interactions and their implications.
