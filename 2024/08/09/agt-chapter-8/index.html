# Game Theory

1. Cryptographic Notions and Settings

The goal of Multi-Party Computation (MPC) is to allow multiple parties, each with their private inputs, to jointly compute a function such that: - Correctness: Each party learns the correct output. - Privacy: No party learns more about the other parties' inputs than what is necessary.

  1. Key Generalizations:
  • Probabilistic Functions:
    • Functions where the output depends on a random string , which is not controlled by any party but is jointly generated.
  • Multioutput Functions:
    • Functions where each party receives a unique output , and no party learns about the other parties' inputs or outputs.
  1. Roles of Parties in Cryptographic Protocols:
  • Honest: Follow the protocol without deviation.
  • Faulty:
    • Honest-but-curious: Follow the protocol but try to learn as much as possible from the process.
    • Malicious: Actively deviate from the protocol to influence the outcome or learn unauthorized information.

An adversary controlling the faulty parties can collude to compromise the computation, but the security of the MPC protocol can be maintained as long as the number of corrupted parties is below a certain threshold.

1.1 Security of Multiparty Computations

  1. Objective: This section formulates what it means to securely compute a function within the context of multiparty computation (MPC). The security of MPC protocols is established by comparing the real execution of the protocol with an ideal model, where a trusted third party computes the function.

  2. Key Concepts:

  • Ideal Model:
    • A trusted party privately receives the inputs from all parties, computes the function , and returns the output to each party.
    • Ensures correctness (correct output is computed) and privacy (no additional information is revealed).
  • Real Model:
    • Parties interact according to a protocol to compute without a trusted third party.
    • The protocol is considered secure if the real execution of the protocol does not expose more information than in the ideal model.
  1. Definition 8.1:
  • A protocol is said to securely compute a function if, for every adversary in the real model, there exists a corresponding adversary in the ideal model such that the distribution of outputs (both for the honest parties and the adversary) in the real model is indistinguishable from the distribution in the ideal model.
  1. Variations on Security:
  • Output Delivery:
    • Ensures all honest parties receive the output.
  • Fairness:
    • If at least one honest party receives the output, then all honest parties should eventually receive it.
  • Correctness and Privacy:
    • Faulty parties may learn their outputs and block others, but the protocol still guarantees that the outputs learned are correct and that the privacy of inputs is maintained.
  1. Key Assumptions:
  • Party Behavior:
    • Parties may be honest-but-curious or malicious, and security assumptions must account for these behaviors.
  • Computational Assumptions:
    • Security definitions depend on whether the adversary is computationally bounded or unbounded, with concepts like perfect security and statistical security introduced.
  • Communication Assumptions:
    • Assumes secure channels or broadcast channels for communication among parties.
    • Uses idealized concepts like envelopes for secure message storage and delivery.

2. Game Theory Notions and Settings

  1. Strategic Games:
    • The section begins by assuming familiarity with basic concepts of game theory, particularly Nash Equilibrium (NE) and Correlated Equilibrium (CE).
    • Nash Equilibrium: Refers to independent strategies where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy.
    • Correlated Equilibrium: Allows for correlated strategies, which typically require a mediator (correlation device) to sample and recommend strategies to the players. Each player receives only their recommended action and learns nothing about others' strategies.
  2. Games with Incomplete Information:
    • These are games where each player has a private type , drawn from a known distribution.
    • The utility of each player depends not only on the actions of all players but also on their private types and the joint type distribution of all players.
    • The concept of Bayesian Nash Equilibrium is introduced for such games, where players maximize their expected utility based on their beliefs about the types of other players.
  3. Mediated Games with Types:
    • This extends the notion of mediated games to scenarios where players must send their types to the mediator before receiving their recommended actions.
    • Players are expected to honestly report their types to the mediator. However, they may deviate by sending false types or not sending their types at all.
    • Correlated Equilibrium for Incomplete Information: The concept is generalized to consider games where players' strategies depend on their private types, even for types outside the support of the type distribution.
  4. Aborting the Game:
    • The section discusses the possibility of incorporating an "abort" action, where the game can be stopped, leading to predefined payoffs.
    • Although not all games include this action, it's a relevant consideration for certain strategic settings.
  5. Extended Games:
    • Extended Games: These involve a sequence of moves rather than a single-shot decision, typically starting with a cheap-talk phase where players communicate to establish strategies before playing the actual game.
    • Cheap-Talk Phase: This phase allows players to discuss strategies without any binding commitments. The strategies discussed here influence the subsequent game phase.
  6. Nash Equilibrium in Extended Games:
    • The concept of Nash Equilibrium is extended to these more complex games. However, there is a recognition that traditional Nash Equilibrium may be too permissive for extensive form games, as it allows for "unreasonable" equilibria with empty threats.
  7. Collusions:
    • The discussion transitions to collusions, which are typically not considered in noncooperative game theory.
    • Cooperative Game Theory: In contrast, this branch of game theory models scenarios where agents can form collusions, though traditional solutions in cooperative games are often weak.
    • The section foreshadows a discussion in Section 8.4.1 on a new equilibrium concept influenced by the MPC setting that may address these collusion scenarios more effectively.

3. Contrasting MPC and Games

In Section 8.3, the similarities and differences between Multi-Party Computation (MPC) and game theory are explored. Both fields involve computing a function with private inputs, but they differ significantly in their approaches and underlying principles.

  1. Incentives and Rationality
    • Game Theory:
      • Heavily relies on incentives. Players are assumed to be rational—they aim to maximize their utility. Rationality is also common knowledge, meaning all players know that others are rational and use this knowledge in their decision-making.
    • MPC:
      • Incentives are typically external to the computation. The focus is on ensuring correct and meaningful outputs even if participants behave maliciously.
  2. Privacy and Solution Drivers
    • Cryptography (MPC):
      • The goal is to achieve a secure protocol as defined earlier in the chapter. Privacy is of utmost importance, and the protocol ensures that participants learn nothing beyond their own input and output.
    • Game Theory:
      • The goal is to achieve equilibrium—a stable state where no player has an incentive to deviate from their strategy. Privacy is often less of a concern compared to achieving a stable outcome.
  3. Crime and Punishment
    • Cryptography:
      • Focuses on security despite deviations. The goal is to compute the function correctly, even if some parties deviate. Punishment in this context might involve excluding the deviating party from the computation, but the primary goal is to ensure the computation’s completion.
    • Game Theory:
      • Emphasizes specifying how misbehavior will be dealt with by other parties. Punishment strategies are designed to ensure that deviations are not in the best interest of any player.
  4. Efficiency
    • Game Theory:
      • Typically does not impose computational limits on players when deciding whether to deviate.
    • MPC:
      • Often assumes computational limits on adversaries, designing protocols to withstand only computationally bounded attacks.
  5. Collusions
    • Game Theory:
      • Typically considers a non-cooperative setting, where collusions of size are the norm.
    • MPC:
      • Collusion tolerance is a significant focus. In many MPC settings, protocols can tolerate collusions up to a linear fraction of the total number of parties, sometimes even up to all but one party.

4. Cryptographic Influences on Game Theory

4.1 New Notions

This section introduces new equilibrium concepts in game theory that are directly influenced by cryptographic techniques, specifically from the field of Multi-Party Computation (MPC). Two key notions are discussed: computational equilibrium and -resilient equilibrium.

  1. Computational Equilibrium
    • Definition: Computational equilibrium considers settings where parties are computationally bounded, meaning their strategies and any possible deviations are limited by computational constraints (parameterized by a security parameter ).
    • Scenario:
      • The concept assumes the existence of computationally hard problems, such that any efficient strategy a player might use is bound by the same computational limitations.
      • Even if an attacker could potentially break the underlying problem, the expected gain from such a break is negligible.

  2. -Resiliency

    • -Resilient Nash Equilibrium:
      • A strategy profile is a -resilient Nash equilibrium if, for any coalition of up to players and for any deviation strategy of the coalition, the utility for each player in is not improved by the deviation.
    • -Resilient Correlated Equilibrium:
      • This concept extends to correlated strategies, with two variants:
        • Ex Ante: Coalition members collude before receiving their recommended actions, agreeing on a joint deviation strategy.
        • Interim: Coalition members see their recommended actions first, then decide whether to deviate together.
  3. Implications
    • Strength of Cryptographic Techniques:
      • The introduction of such strong equilibria showcases the power of cryptographic approaches in ensuring stable and resilient outcomes in game-theoretic scenarios.
    • Applicability:
      • These new notions can be particularly useful in settings involving incomplete information or where robust cooperation against coalitions is critical.

4.3: Stronger Equilibria

  1. Objective: This section addresses the need for stronger and more stable equilibria beyond standard Nash equilibria, particularly in the context of extensive-form games. The focus is on developing equilibria that eliminate weaknesses like empty threats and better simulate the correlated equilibria of the original game.

  2. Key Concepts:

    • Eliminating Empty Threats:
      • Problem with Nash Equilibria: Nash equilibria can allow for empty threats, which are strategies that threaten actions not credible because they are detrimental to the party issuing the threat.
      • Solution: One way to eliminate empty threats is to modify punishment strategies. For example, instead of threatening a min-max punishment (which might be harmful to the punisher as well), the strategy could be to revert to the worst Nash equilibrium for the deviating party. This approach still constitutes an equilibrium of the game but is more credible.
    • Ex Ante Correlated Equilibria:
      • This subsection explores the challenge of simulating ex ante correlated equilibria, where players collude before receiving their recommended actions. This type of equilibrium is more challenging to simulate because it requires preventing any subliminal communication (steganography) between the colluding parties.
      • Solution: Certain cryptographic protocols (such as those involving randomness or entropy) can be used to design collusion-free protocols that can simulate ex ante correlated equilibria without a mediator.
    • Iterated Deletion of Weakly Dominated Strategies:
      • The section references a method to achieve stronger equilibria by focusing on strategies that survive the iterated deletion of weakly dominated strategies, leading to more robust outcomes.
    • Strategic and Privacy Equivalence:
      • Strategic Equivalence: Ensures that the simulation maintains the same strategic power as the original game.
      • Privacy Equivalence: Guarantees that the simulation preserves the privacy of players' types as effectively as the ideal model.

  3. Conclusion:

    • MPC Techniques as a Substitute for Mediators: While MPC techniques are promising for replacing mediators through cheap talk in various settings, achieving stronger equilibria remains challenging, particularly under weaker assumptions. Further research is needed to strengthen these techniques and ensure more stable equilibria in complex game-theoretic scenarios.
